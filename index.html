Cosmetics as Politics &
Politics of Cosmetics

A Multimedia Experience Based on Kathy Peiss’s Book
Hope in a Jar: The Making of America’s Beauty Culture

The ideal image of women constantly changes across different time and places. Cosmetics have played a critical role in shaping women’s identity throughout the history.


Although nowadays using cometics is sometimes condemned as a source of women’s oppression, a century ago, it embodied women's self-expression and emancipation. The cosmetic industry opened up opportunities for women to achieve their ambitions, build communities and empower other women.


By reflecting on the cosmetics history, at times beautiful and at times ugly, the beauty culture may push forward to more inclusivity, diversity and relevance for a new era.

A Pursuit of
“Natural” Pale
Complexion

For 19th-century Americans, the act of beautifying was frowned upon as it was seen as immoral and unrespectable. The “painted woman” who put on makeup provoked disgust and censure from the virtuous. Only stage actress and prostitutes, as it was generally agreed, would enhance their face with artifice.

One woman was "painting" her face while the other was surprised and uncomfortable. Full video.



In contrast, a lady of a higher social standing would avoid using visible makeup and pursue a “natural” pale look instead. A pale complexion was preferred as it would indicate that a woman did not have to work outside, and was thus of higher status. At that time, they would even use accessories like gloves and umbrellas to help protect them from the sunshine.

Victorian ladies used hats, gloves and umbrellas to protect themselves from the sun. Image 1, Image 2.




Apart from covering their skin with clothes, Victorian ladies also secretly used some cosmetics that would enhance their pale look. Many cosmetic products then were aimed at achieving as pale a complexion as possible. Cold cream, vanishing cream, lotion, etc. became a staple in the beauty rituals of Victorian women.


Newspaper ads that promoted "pearly white", "transparent" and "clear" complexion. Read more.



However, it's worth noting that some substances in the cosmetics were corrosive to the skin because they contained lead, mercury, and arsenic -- all harmful chemicals. Women would have to keep applying them to cover the damage the products caused.

An ad showing a product containing arsenic, a toxic compound that would cause health issues with long-term exposure. Read more.






Rejecting the
Victorian
Womanhood

The New Women image was often positioned in opposition to the image of Victorian ladies, whose essential concept of femininity was domesticity and submissiveness.

Unlike them, new Women went out to the public as professional woman, earnest labor activist, the free-spirited outdoor girl and sexually assertive flapper, etc.

From then on, the demand for cosmetics increased and the act of beautifying was associated with women’s social life, activities, and independence from the past era.



Switchboard operators, circa 1925. Source.




With the impact of WWI, by 1920, more and more women entered the job market. They dressed up, applied makeup and got ready to take over the jobs from men who had gone to war.

Image 1: a woman at a milk bottling plant in the 1920s. Image 2: workers carried freshly manufactured tennis balls. Source.




Representations of the political New Woman in the media, whether as a suffragist, feminist, or social reformer, often portrayed her as masculine, unattractively clothed in bloomers, and sometimes smoking.

Image 1: Frances Benjamin Johnston's Self-Portrait (as "New Woman"). Image 2: a woman in male attire, smoking a cigarette and supervising a man who did the laundry in a dress and an apron. Image 1, Image 2.



In contrst to Victorian ladies who constraint themselves at home, New Women embraced a healthier lifestyle. They went out for outdoor activities, had fun and got tanned.

A group of women on a golf course, circa 1920. Source.






Redefining
Femininity

Flappers were also one type of New Woman. Specifically, they were the icons of the Roaring 20's, who challenged the traditional Victorian gender norms by wearing excessive makeup, drinking alcohol, smoking cigarettes in public, driving automobiles, and treating sex in a casual manner.

Flapper girls putting on makeup, dancing with jazz and reading newspapers.
Full video.


They defied the convention with their makeup, clothes, hair and dance moves. By breaking the gender norms and expectations, they redefined the femininity possible.



Flapper girls putting on makeup, drinking alcohol, dancing with jazz and reading newspapers. Source.



The Roaring 20's marked a new era of automobile advancements. Driving afforded women a freedom and mobility they hadn't experienced before.

A woman setting off in her Humber motor car, circa 1927. Source.




At the meanwhile, the bicycle, also a new technology of its time, had become an enormous cultural and political force, and an emblem of women's rights. To get on a bike, women threw away their long dress, and transformed their toe-to-knee style.

“Find Yourself”

Some ads promoted cosmetics as a tool for women to explore and portray their individuality in the modern world. Some content also raised women’s awareness of sexual freedom, although in the disguise of selling products.






Armand's 1929 campaign, "Find Yourself," offered to lead women to self understanding by analyzing their appearance. It provided a guide to 32 disctinct types of women based on facial appearance.






Although in disguise of selling products, the beauty culturalists used this campaign to encourage women to look into their beauty and find their “true self-image”.

Full video can be found here.




Most of the ads proclaimed the mutual transformation of external appearance and inner well-being. “Before-and after” imagery appeared in ads conveying that women could not only change her looks but remake their life.






ln a 1924 trade advertisement for Zip depilatory, a dark-skinned woman, with the appearance of an eastern or southern European immigrant, achieved social acceptance in America by ridding herself of superfluous hair.











“Fire & Ice”

Fire and Ice, a Revlon lipstick color introduced in 1952 -- “for you who love to flirt with fire, who dare to skate upon thin ice”-- changed the sexual resonance of cosmetics advertising.




Accompanying with the photograph of a beautiful model with a sequined gown, it had a questionnaire for customers to check if they were “ready for Fire and Ice.”





It asked some questions about traditional heterosexual romance but also quizzed women on their desire for sensuous pleasure, and their advertuousness.

“Do you dance with your shoes off?”

“If tourist flights were running would you take a trip to Mars?”

There was no heterosexual encounter, no romance scene, just a self-absorbed woman. To some degree, it encouraged women’s sexual freedom and presented cosmetics as a playful activity.






"Ideal Wife"

Many magazine ads in the 50s emphasized the effect cosmetics would have on men. Ads were showing flawless beauty, made consumption integral to achieving the feminine ideal.






They promoted a perfect wife image who “leads a double life”, who takes care of kitchen but could still remain attractive for their husbands.






In the postwar period, sexual allure and desire were celebrated as attributes of normal female psyche. A woman acted upon her desire for a man by making herself beautiful, in order to awaken his desire.

The sexist content and image aimed to entice women customers and raise their insecurities, which added to another layer of psychological interpretation of cosmetics.










White-Washed
Content

Most mid-century ads not only trapped in male gaze, but also failed to represent women of different ethnic backgrounds. The single representation of ideal beauty, including the hair look, the cosmetic style, the skin color tone, etc. was white washed.




The limited representation of diverse female beauty confined women’s aesthetic choices. This resulted in mounting discontent among African Americans, feminists, and gay activists who challenged the commercial exploitation of the governing beauty ideals afterwards.

Red Lipsticks
as a Sign of Solidarity


Due to the endorsement of leading suffragists, lipstick became a symbol for female emancipation.

Leaders such as Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Charlotte Perkins Gilman trumpeted the wearing of lip rouge as an emblem of women’s emancipation, and incorporated its use into the 1912 New York Suffragette Rally. Thereafter, suffragists wore a particularly noticeable shade of red lip rouge as part of the standard rally procedure.




In both America and England, women publicly applied lip rouge with the express intent of appalling men. Lipstick’s long proscription by social, religious, and legal male authority made it a ready symbol for female rebellion.



Beauty Empire
for Black Women


Madam C. J. Walker (1867-1919) was “the first black woman millionaire in America” and made her fortune thanks to her homemade line of hair care products for black women. Born Sarah Breedlove to parents who had been slaves, she was inspired to create her hair products after an experience with hair loss, which led to the creation of the “Walker system” of hair care.




A talented entrepreneur with a knack for self-promotion, Walker built a business empire, at first selling products directly to black women, then employing “beauty culturalists” to hand-sell her wares. The self-made millionaire used her fortune to fund scholarships for women at the Tuskegee Institute and donated large parts of her wealth to the NAACP, the black YMCA and other charities.




Both Madam C. J. Walker and Annie Tumbo Malone refused to sell skin bleach and emphasized hair care, not straightening, in their beauty systems. Building their beauty empire, they created thousands of job opportunities for black women and continuously gave back to the community



Drag Makeup
to Disrupt Gender Norms

Drag is a gender-bending art form in which a person dresses in clothing and makeup meant to exaggerate a specific gender identity, usually of the opposite sex.

Drag has deep roots in Western culture, specifically in theatre when women weren’t permitted to perform on stage so men would play female roles. Drag performers were featured as far back as ancient Greece and carried on through Shakespearean times.

Two men crossdressing and playing in Shakespeare's best-loved comedy,
Twelfth Night. Full video.



Cross-dressing and drag performances existed as an underground culture for much of the 20th century, and the visibility of drag queens increased alongside that of LGBT communities in the late 1960s.

The Harlem Renaissance (1920-1935) was particularly influential to this process. Through dance, drag queens showed how gender is a performance – they pretended to put on makeup or “beat face”, style their hair, and put on extravagant clothes.


Drag queens at the Harlem drag balls. Source.



In Cristy’s (2017) interviews with drag queens to understand their gender performance, the participants mentioned they attained social and political visibility through drag, as drag helped them reach a larger audience when attempting to express social and political issues and norms. One mentioned that “drag is a political tool”.


The action of painting and applying makeup has been a critical way for drag performers to shape their subculture, and with that, challenge geneder roles. Being queer disrupts heteronormative expectations, and drag is an extension of this disruption.

A drag queen during the Stonewall riots. Source.





About the project


This is a capstone project for Huan's master study in Digital Media at Georgia Tech. By bringing back the rich history of cosmetics with a digital museum experience, this project hopes to uncover the power of cosmetics as a political tool to challenge some mainstream views.

The main argument was based on Kathy Peiss's book Hope in a Jar: The Making of America’s Beauty Culture. Therefore, this project also functions as a multimedia remediation for the reading experience to capture and share history in an engaging and informative way.
